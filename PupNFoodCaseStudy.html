Case Study

Pup 'N Food

Project Overview


The Goal:

Design a dog food ordering/delivery app that allows users to better customize their notifications and delivery preferences for their order.

My Role:

Lead UX Designer for the hypothetical dog food delivery company called Pup 'N Food from conception to delivery.

  • Responsibilities

    • Conducting interviews
    • Paper and digital wireframing
    • Low and high-fidelity prototyping
    • Conducting usability studies
    • Accounting for accessibility
    • Iterating on designs


    • Understanding the User

    Persona:

    Rhonda is a parent of a toddler and dog owner who needs to accurately predict when their dog food will be delivered because they need to put their dogs away so that they do not bark and wake up the toddler.

    User Journey:

    Mapping Rhonda's user journey revealed how helpful it would be for users to be able to adjust notification preferences for delivery updates to better anticipate their order delivery.

  • User Research - Pain Points:

    • Inaccurate Delivery Estimates - When users expect for an order to come at a certain time and it doesn't, it causes frustration.
    • Limited Types of Notifications - Users often only have one time of notification option for delivery, namely email. Email notification settings are not always instantaneous on user phones.
    • Limited Frequency of Notifications - Notification occurrence is often only available for orders being initiated and orders being delivered. Users want to be able to plan around when a delivery will come with additional notification options.


    • Starting the Design

    Sitemap:

    Paper Wireframes:

    In this step, taking the time to draft different iterations of the screen of the website on paper ensured that the elements that made it to digital wireframes would be helpful in addressing user pain points. For the home screen, I prioritized easy access to tracking orders.

    Digital Wireframes:

    Moving from paper to digital wireframes allowed me to utilize the best aspects of various paper wireframes. I prioritized useful button locations on the home page to allow for easy access to previous orders.

    Low-Fidelity Prototype:

    The low-fidelity prototype connected the primary user flow of viewing items on the home page, shopping, viewing item details, viewing the cart, and tracking their order.


  • Usability Study - Findings:

    Round 1:

    1. (1) Users want navigation to previous orders to be prioritized.
    2. (2) Users want to be able to navigate to the homepage by clicking the logo.
    3. (3) Users want more cues for when they've added an item to their cart.


    Round 2:

    1. (1) Users need to be able to do a "quick add" without leaving the shopping page.
    2. (2) Users need to have an alternate way of interacting with the preferences on mobile (not check boxes).

    • Refining the Design

    Mockups:

    To make the process of adding items to the cart easier for users, a "quick add" feature was added on the shopping page where users can edit the quantity.

    Device Considerations and Responsive Design

    Since this website has customers who access the site on a variety of devices, I created alternate designs for additional screen sizes to make sure the site would be fully responsive.

    To improve the process of editing preferences on a mobile device, button toggle interaction replaced checkboxes on the mobile site.

    Mockups: Desktop Website



  • Accessibility Considerations:

    1. (1) I used headings with different sized text for clear visual hierarchy.
    2. (2) Colors for buttons, backgrounds, and text have been adjusted to promote accessibility.
    3. (3) Layouts have been designed to be consistent. All call to action buttons have the same size, shape, and color to promote accessibility.
