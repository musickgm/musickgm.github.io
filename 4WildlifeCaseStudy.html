Case Study

4 Wildlife

Project Overview


The Goal:

Design a crowdsourced science application and website to assist with collecting data on endangered species populations.

My Role:

Lead UX Designer for the hypothetical conservation non-profit called "4 Wildlife" from conception to delivery.

  • Responsibilities

    • Conducting interviews
    • Paper and digital wireframing
    • Low and high-fidelity prototyping
    • Conducting usability studies
    • Accounting for accessibility
    • Iterating on designs


    • Understanding the User

    Persona:

    Elena is a young professional who needs a way of reporting sightings of endangered wildlife while on hikes in order to contribute to saving endangered species.

    User Journey:

    Mapping Elena's user journey revealed how helpful it would be for users to be able to take and upload pictures without cell service.

  • User Research - Pain Points:

    • Inability to Assist - When users encounter an endangered species in the wild, they do not know what to do or how to assist scientists with conservation efforts.
    • Poor Communication - Community outreach is limited as users do not know about conservation events in their area or how effective conservation efforts are.
    • Poor Cell Service When on Hikes - When on hikes, there is often poor cell service. Users do not have a way to upload species encounter data to the website.


    • Starting the Design

    Sitemap:

    Paper Wireframes:

    In this step, taking the time to draft different iterations of the screen of the app on paper ensured that the elements that made it to digital wireframes would be helpful in addressing user pain points. For the home screen, I prioritized easy access to logging encounters.

    Digital Wireframes:

    Moving from paper to digital wireframes allowed me to utilize the best aspects of various paper wireframes. I prioritized displaying information about nearby species and a button to log any encounters.

    Low-Fidelity Prototype:

    The low-fidelity prototype connected the primary user flow of viewing endangered species, logging an encounter, and a confirmation page.


  • Usability Study - Findings:

    1. (1) Users need a button on every page to return to the home page.
    2. (2) Users need to be able to edit the date and location of a sighting log.
    3. (3) Users need an easily accessible button on the home page to log sightings.

    • Refining the Design

    Mockups:

    Based on insights from the usability study, the "log species encounter" button is more prominent on the home page. Also, an icon that links to the home page was added on each page.

    Device Considerations and Responsive Design

    An app and website version of the service was created with different priorities. The mobile app prioritizes logging species encounters while the desktop website prioritizes education and donations.

  • Accessibility Considerations:

    1. (1) I used headings with different sized text for clear visual hierarchy.
    2. (2) Colors for buttons, backgrounds, and text have been adjusted to promote accessibility.
    3. (3) Layouts have been designed to be consistent. All call to action buttons have the same size, shape, and color to promote accessibility.
